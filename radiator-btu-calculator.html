How We Calculate Radiator BTU

The radiator calculator begins with room volume because hydronic and electric radiators must offset heat loss through walls, windows, ceilings, and floors. We then adjust for room type, external walls, glazing quality, floor exposure, insulation level, and system temperature. Lower flow temperatures such as 50°C require larger emitters because the radiator releases less heat at lower water temperatures.

Results are shown in both BTU and watts so you can compare UK-style radiator catalogues and metric output tables. When the room is large, the calculator suggests using two radiators for more even distribution.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I calculate the BTU of my radiator?

Measure the room, estimate its heat loss, and compare the result against the published radiator output at the same system temperature, usually ΔT50 or manufacturer-specific ratings.

How many BTU does a radiator need for a bedroom?

Many bedrooms need roughly 2,500 to 4,500 BTU, but insulation, glazing, ceiling height, and room exposure can change that significantly.

How to calculate BTU for a room in meters?

Use the ft/m toggle for room dimensions. The calculator converts metric dimensions automatically before applying the room heat-loss formula.

What BTU radiator for a 15m² room?

A 15 square meter room often lands around 3,500 to 5,000 BTU depending on insulation and exposure. Bathrooms and conservatories usually need more.
