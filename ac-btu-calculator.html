AC BTU Calculation Formula

The standard formula for air conditioner BTU is based on ACCA Manual J methodology:

Basic cooling BTU formula:

BTU = Sq Ft × 20 × Climate Factor × Insulation Factor

Then add adjustment factors for ceiling height, sun, occupants, and kitchen.

Step-by-step example

  • 1. Room: 20 ft × 15 ft = 300 sq ft
  • 2. Base BTU: 300 × 20 = 6,000 BTU
  • 3. Hot climate (TX): × 1.20 = 7,200 BTU
  • 4. Average insulation: × 1.00 = 7,200 BTU
  • 5. 10 ft ceiling (÷8 × 1.25): = 9,000 BTU
  • 6. Sunny south-facing: + 10% = ~10,000 BTU

All Adjustment Factors

Climate Multipliers

  • Hot (FL/TX/AZ): × 1.20
  • Mixed (Midwest): × 1.05
  • Moderate (Pacific): × 0.95
  • Cool (Northeast): × 0.85
  • Very cold: × 0.80

Insulation Multipliers

  • Poor (pre-1970): × 1.15
  • Average (1970–2000): × 1.00
  • Good (2000–2015): × 0.90
  • Excellent (2015+): × 0.82

Sun Exposure Adjustments

  • Heavy shade: – 15%
  • Mostly shaded: – 10%
  • Normal: + 0%
  • Sunny: + 10%
  • Very sunny: + 20%

Other Adjustments (Add)

  • Each extra occupant: + 600 BTU
  • Kitchen nearby: + 4,000 BTU
  • Top floor / under roof: + 15%
  • Basement room: – 10%
  • Ceiling above 8 ft: × (h ÷ 8)
  • Each large window: + 1,000 BTU

Source: US DOE Air Conditioning Guidelines & ACCA Manual J.

AC BTU Calculator — FAQ

Answers to the most common air conditioner BTU questions.

What is the BTU formula for air conditioners?

Basic formula: BTU = Room sq ft × 20 × Climate Factor × Insulation Factor

Key adjustments on top of the base:

  • Ceiling above 8 ft: multiply base by (height ÷ 8)
  • Sunny room: add 10% — shaded room: subtract 10%
  • Each occupant beyond 2: add 600 BTU
  • Kitchen: add 4,000 BTU
  • Top floor / under roof: add 15%

See the full BTU calculation guide for worked examples.

How many BTU do I need for a 12×12 room?

A 12×12 room (144 sq ft) needs 5,000–6,000 BTU for cooling.

  • Moderate climate, average insulation: 5,000–5,500 BTU
  • Hot climate (FL, TX, AZ): 6,000–7,000 BTU
  • Sunny room or poor insulation: +10–15%
  • Basement / shaded: 4,500–5,000 BTU

A 5,000 BTU window AC is the standard recommendation for a 12×12 bedroom.

What size window air conditioner do I need for 300 sq ft?

A 300 sq ft room needs approximately 7,000–8,000 BTU for a window air conditioner.

  • Moderate climate, average insulation: 7,000 BTU
  • Hot climate or sunny room: 8,000–9,000 BTU
  • Poor insulation or top floor: 9,000–10,000 BTU

Standard 8,000 BTU window AC units are the best fit for most 300 sq ft rooms.

Is it better to oversize or undersize an air conditioner?

Neither is ideal, but oversizing is the bigger problem.

An oversized AC "short-cycles" — it cools the air temperature quickly but shuts off before dehumidifying properly. This leaves rooms feeling clammy, uses more energy, and causes premature wear.

An undersized AC runs constantly but can't reach setpoint temperature on hot days. Maximum safe oversizing: no more than 10–15% above calculated BTU.

What SEER rating should I look for in a new AC?

As of 2023, the US minimum is SEER2 13 (South/Southwest) or SEER2 14 (North). ENERGY STAR requires SEER2 ≥ 15.

  • Window AC: look for EER ≥ 12
  • Central AC: SEER2 ≥ 16 recommended
  • Mini split: SEER 18–22+ for best efficiency

Higher SEER is worth it in warm climates — the payback period is 5–8 years in areas running AC 6+ months per year.

How many BTU for a 1,500 sq ft house?

A 1,500 sq ft home typically needs 24,000–30,000 BTU (2–2.5 tons) of central air conditioning.

  • Cool climate (Pacific NW, NE): 21,000–24,000 BTU
  • Moderate climate (Midwest): 24,000–27,000 BTU
  • Hot climate (TX, FL, AZ): 28,000–36,000 BTU

For mini split coverage, use multiple zones totalling the same BTU. Use the Mini Split BTU Calculator for multi-zone sizing.

Can a portable AC cool the same as a window AC of the same BTU?

No — portable ACs are less efficient than window units of the same listed BTU.

The DOE's SACC (Seasonally Adjusted Cooling Capacity) rating reflects real-world output. A 10,000 BTU portable AC typically has a SACC of 6,000–7,000 BTU. Size a portable unit 25–35% larger than your calculated BTU when using listed (not SACC) ratings.

For a 300 sq ft room needing 8,000 BTU: choose a 10,000–12,000 BTU portable AC.

How do I calculate BTU for an open floor plan?

For open floor plans, calculate the total square footage of the connected area as one room.

If a living room (400 sq ft) flows into a kitchen (200 sq ft), calculate for 600 sq ft total, then add the +4,000 BTU kitchen adjustment.

For multi-story open plans, calculate each floor separately — heat rises, so upper floors need more cooling BTU.
