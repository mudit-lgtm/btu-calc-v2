How We Calculate Heating BTU

The heating calculator uses a simplified Manual J approach. It starts with a BTU-per-square-foot factor tied to climate severity, then modifies that baseline for insulation level, window count, and ceiling height. Multi-story homes get a small additional factor because stack effect and extra envelope area can increase demand. For fossil-fuel equipment such as gas furnaces, propane furnaces, or oil boilers, the output is adjusted by AFUE efficiency so you can compare delivered heat to input capacity.

This method is ideal for planning replacement systems, comparing heat pump sizes, or estimating annual heating cost. For final design, include infiltration testing, duct losses, and room-by-room load analysis.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many BTU do I need to heat a 1,500 sq ft house?

Many 1,500 square foot homes need roughly 37,500 to 75,000 BTU depending on climate, insulation, and windows. Cold climates and older homes land at the high end.

What size furnace do I need for a 2,000 sq ft home?

A 2,000 square foot home often needs about 50,000 to 100,000 BTU of furnace capacity depending on your climate and building shell. Oversizing is common, so use calculated loads first.

How to calculate BTU for gas furnace?

Multiply square footage by a climate-based BTU per square foot factor, then adjust for insulation, windows, and ceiling height. Divide by AFUE efficiency to estimate furnace input size.

How many BTU per square foot for heating?

A practical range is 20 to 50 BTU per square foot. Hot climates and efficient homes need the low end, while very cold climates and leaky homes need the high end.

What is the formula for heating BTU calculation?

A simple form is square feet × climate factor × shell adjustments. More advanced methods add infiltration, room-by-room envelope data, and duct or hydronic distribution losses.
